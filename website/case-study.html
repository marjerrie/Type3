Initially choosing a topic for this project was difficult. It was imperative to me to choose something I felt deeply for. I couldn’t just arbitrarily pick a topic that was “meaningful.” It needed to resonate with me on a deeper level and figuring that out required some digging.

Despite the, what seemed to be, ever changing topic, everything I wanted to work with involved the idea of identity. Originally, I had decided to work with first-generation Asian Americans and how growing up with Eastern ideals in a Western world had affected their personal sense of self. I chose this because it was and still is something I continually battle with. However, it proved itself to be too extensive of a topic to work with in the time span we were given and ultimately, I wasn’t confident enough with this topic to keep working with it.

I eventually realized that the only way to move forward was to focus on Filipino Americans because that’s a more complete part of my identity than anything else making the connection to my work much more instant and prominent. What sparked this realization was Anthony Ocampo’s The Latinos of Asia. I came across this title in my search for relevant information on the preceding idea. I had been jumping back and forth between staying broad or honing my content for weeks, but finding this solidified my intent.

By reading through several articles and listening to interviews on this book, I fostered the idea of discussing how the ambivalent nature of the Filipino race—the ambivalence rooted in our culture’s over 400+ years of unwanted colonization first by Spain in 1521 and later by the U.S. in 1898—has affected one’s sense of identity with fellow peers between the ages of 18-25.

As I started having conversations with others, however, my topic organically transformed into something much more powerful. Everyone I’ve spoken with all seem yearn to feel a stronger connection to our Filipino heritage. This broken connection had a greater prevalence to one’s sense of identity than the ambivalence of our race.

My natural reaction was to create a book with the content I acquired, but through time it didn’t seem enough. Even though a book would’ve still been able to reach a large audience, it felt too contained and confined. I needed to share this on a larger scale and thus creating a website became appropriate. On the other hand, some of the words and stories I collected became too precious to live on-screen. Reading them online would communicate a different mood and thus an accompanying zine that readers would receive by signing up for the website’s newsletter was produced.

Bagong Sarili seeks to start a conversation amongst young adults about our identities as Filipino Americans as well as create a community empowering people to explore and learn more about our Filipino heritage in order to establish a stronger connection to the culture.
